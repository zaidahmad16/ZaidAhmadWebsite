# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Two audiences, weighted equally:

**Recruiters and hiring managers** at software/systems-engineering companies. They scan fast: does this person have real projects, real depth, and can they communicate what they built? They leave the portfolio with a yes/no on whether to move to a screen call.

**Technical peers and collaborators** — other developers, potential open source collaborators, grad program reviewers. They read the project write-ups. They notice whether the person understands what they're talking about at the system level.

One sentence both audiences should walk away thinking: "This person goes further than asked."

## Product Purpose

A personal portfolio for Zaid Ahmad, CS student at Carleton University (AI/ML co-op stream, 2024–2029), specializing in systems programming and embedded development. Projects range from a bare-metal STM32 bootloader to a Linux kernel wireless patch to a full-stack course mapping tool used by 800+ students.

Success: a recruiter books a screen call. A technical reader adds the GitHub to their bookmarks.

## Brand Personality

Sharp · Technical · Direct

The voice is confident without being loud. The aesthetic reflects the same precision you find in kernel code: nothing unnecessary, everything load-bearing. The site doesn't perform "developer" — it demonstrates it through the quality of the work and the quality of the writing about the work.

## Anti-references

- **Generic Tailwind starter**: hero + 3-equal-card feature grid + dark navbar template look. The AI-scaffolded default.
- **Resume-on-a-page**: dense timeline with no visual hierarchy or project depth. The site should be a portfolio, not a PDF.
- **Hacker / terminal aesthetic**: green-on-black, ASCII borders, monochrome terminal cosplay. Too on-the-nose for someone who actually works at the systems level. The real thing doesn't need to announce itself.
- **Dribbble portfolio**: gradient blobs, animated SVGs, big colorful illustrations. Design-school-flavored; wrong register for a systems/embedded dev.

## Design Principles

1. **The work earns attention.** Every design decision directs eyes toward the projects and the writing, not toward the visual wrapper. Decoration that doesn't serve the content gets cut.
2. **Systems thinking in the aesthetic.** The design reflects the same discipline as the projects: nothing arbitrary, clear hierarchy, consistent tokens. The medium and the message align.
3. **Depth over breadth.** Each project gets enough space to communicate what was actually hard about it. Skimming is fast; reading rewards.
4. **Legible before beautiful.** Contrast, type hierarchy, and white space are non-negotiable. Beauty follows from clarity, not in place of it.
5. **Neither template nor trick.** The site shouldn't look like a starter kit or a visual gimmick. The aesthetic should be genuinely considered, in the same way the code is.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

- WCAG 2.1 AA minimum. Body text at ≥4.5:1 contrast, large/bold text at ≥3:1.
- Full keyboard navigation for all interactive elements.
- Respect `prefers-reduced-motion`: animations off or crossfade-only when the user requests it.
- Single-color accent system (no reliance on color alone to convey meaning).
